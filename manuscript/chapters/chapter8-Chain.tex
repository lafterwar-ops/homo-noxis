        % 1- Arrival in the room, atmosphere

The room was small and smelled of oil, dust, metal, old paper, and mistakes.
It was a workroom, low-ceilinged and warm, with walls blackened by lamp smoke and shelves crowded so heavily that they appeared to have survived by mutual pressure.
There were lenses wrapped in cloth, brass tubes, old diagrams, trays of powdered glass, a cracked bowl, lengths of wire, mirror shards, sealed jars, folded papers, two blackened spoons, a small hand-lamp whose flame was turned downward into water, and an object that resembled the skeleton of a bird made by someone who had never seen a bird but had heard disturbing rumours.\par

Va-Raedin stood near the table.
Ka-Syphiron sat behind it, not in a formal chair but on a low stool with one uneven leg.
He was wearing no ceremonial outer robe.
His sleeves were rolled to the wrist.
A dark mark crossed one finger, either soot or ink or blood long dried.
He looked less like one of the five Keepers of the Mountain than like a man who had been interrupted in the middle of ruining something interesting.\par

“Close the door,” he said and Sa-Kailan obeyed and the latch fell into place with a small sound.\par

For several breaths nobody spoke.
The silence did not resemble the silences of training, where Va-Raedin let an error ripen until the boy who had made it could smell his own stupidity.
This silence had witnesses inside it.
It waited not for an answer, but for sequence.
Ka-Syphiron placed both hands flat on the table.\par

        % 4- Fraternal moment in the chain of light

Then the old Keeper stood, unceremonially.
He simply came around the table, the uneven stool complaining behind him, and placed one hand on Sa-Kailan’s shoulder.
The hand was warm, dry, and surprisingly heavy.\par

“Congratulations, Sa-Kailan. You have done a difficult thing, without making it unbearable to praise you.
That combination is rarer than genius.”\par

Sa-Kailan tried to answer but nothing came except the start of an uncontrollable smile.\par

“And you. Six years.”\par
“There was work.”\par
“There is always work.
That is how unremarkable men hide from glory.”\par

Ka-Syphiron placed his other hand on Va-Raedin’s shoulder.
Now the three of them stood linked beside the table: the Keeper who was about to be released, the Veil who had waited, the Candidate who had opened the lower door.
Nothing in the Mountain’s visible order had yet changed.
No robe had been exchanged, no office named aloud, no Hall warmed, no scripture read.
Yet the chain had already formed in that cramped blackened room, among broken lenses, broken mirrors and piles of forgotten diagrams.\par

“Congratulations, Va-Raedin.”\par
“Keeper.”\par
“No.
Not Keeper.
Not tonight.
Tonight, we celebrate.”\par

Va-Raedin looked up again.
For one absurd breath Sa-Kailan thought his teacher might refuse praise as an imprecision.
Instead Va-Raedin bowed his head.\par

“Thank you.”\par

The words were quiet enough that the room had to earn them.
Ka-Syphiron released them both and stepped back.\par

“There,” he said.
“The chain has now been congratulated in all its necessary links.
If Justice complains tomorrow that joy was not recorded, we shall say the failure was theirs.”\par

Sa-Kailan laughed once.
It came out broken.\par

Va-Raedin looked at him.\par

“Congratulations, well deserved.” he said.\par

The word had not been softened.
Va-Raedin did not know how to soften things without deforming them.
Yet the warmth in it was unmistakable because it had not been offered by accident.\par

“Then he was forbidden to bring water to hearings.”\par

Va-Raedin said, “For seven years.”\par

“For seven productive years.”\par

Sa-Kailan looked from the shutter to the little book.\par

“So motion can be broken.”\par

“Observed in fragments,” Ka-Syphiron said.
“Misassembled by the eye.
Stilled when moving.
Moved when still.
Sight is not a window, but a negotiation with interruption.”\par


    % 19-Cinema second iteration


Ka-Syphiron pushed the shutter aside and turned the black cylinder on its base.\par

“And this one is from five links above us.
Look through the slits.”\par

At first Sa-Kailan saw only interruptions: darkness, image, darkness, image.
Ka-Syphiron turned the handle.
The drum began to spin.
The woman inside shattered into repeated glimpses, each broken by blackness.\par

Then the drum found its rhythm.\par

The woman moved.\par

Cloth loosened.
A shoulder appeared.
The garment slipped.
The sequence reversed and she dressed again, condemned by Light to undress and redress forever inside a spinning cylinder.\par

Sa-Kailan stepped back.\par

“This is obscene.”\par

“And mechanically excellent.”\par

Va-Raedin said, “The second quality is the official reason for its preservation.”\par

“The first,” Ka-Syphiron said, “is why nobody misplaced it.”\par

Va-Raedin looked at Sa-Kailan.\par

“Is he disappointed it is not Va-Sheva?”\par

Sa-Kailan turned violently red.
Ka-Syphiron leaned back in laugther as if struck.\par

“Oh.
That was cleanly done.”\par

Sa-Kailan opened his mouth, found no safe word in any Kahir, and closed it again.\par

“He looks as though he is going to be sick,” Ka-Syphiron said, delighted.\par

“Men are all sick in that regard,” Va-Raedin said.\par

The embarrassing silence that followed was inescapable.\par


    % 20-Archangel joke


Ka-Syphiron lifted one finger.\par

“Speaking of archangels.”\par

Va-Raedin laughed once.\par

“Ha.
Speaking of cleanly done.”\par

“Archangels?” Sa-Kailan said at once, grateful for any path away from Va-Sheva.\par

“When embarassed men have run out of things to say,
they come into this world to suspend time into an awkward silence.”\par

“Light turns stillness into motion,” Ka-Syphiron said.
“Or rather, studies the conditions under which the eye accuses stillness of having moved.”\par


    % 21-The Beast


“And the Beast?”\par

The room stopped breathing.\par

No lamp dimmed.
No cup moved.
Yet something drew inward, as if everything on the shelves had remembered at once that the Mountain was not a toy.\par

Va-Raedin did not look at Ka-Syphiron.

“I only meant—”\par

“No,” Ka-Syphiron said softly.
“You meant exactly what a boy should mean after too much hydromel and not enough terror.
That is not a crime.
Only a condition.”\par

He leaned forward.
His face had not hardened.
That was the frightening part.\par

“You ask whether I believe in the Beast.
Remember, Sa-Kailan, I am one of five who keep it prisoner beneath the Mountain.
On your path to Keepership, if the Mountain does not kill you first, you are bound to make at least one chilling discovery.
One discovery that leaves the room colder than it was before you entered.
One discovery that teaches you the difference between a mystery that invites the mind and a secret that burdens the hand.
And once you do, you will speak like that no more.”\par

Ka-Syphiron’s gaze drifted, for a fraction too small to accuse, toward the stone beneath their feet.
Not down exactly.
Through.
Then it returned.\par

“No office talk tonight.”\par

“But—”\par

“No,” Ka-Syphiron said.
“Especially not if the question begins with but.
But is the hinge on which young men swing themselves into locked rooms.”\par

The Beast remained in the room after the joke ended.
Ka-Syphiron let it remain exactly long enough.
Then he clapped both hands once.\par


    % 22-Justice game


“Nothing better to relax an atmosphere than Justice.”\par

Va-Raedin laughed in disbelief.\par

“You cannot be serious.”\par

“I am almost never serious in ways people recognise at the proper time.”\par

Ka-Syphiron produced a wooden box.
Inside were cards: thin painted slips marked in suits and numbers.\par

“Is this permitted?” Sa-Kailan asked.\par

“By whom?”\par

“Justice.”\par

“Certainly not.”\par

Va-Raedin said, “That is not true.”\par

Ka-Syphiron considered.\par

“No.
It is worse than not true.
It is incomplete.”\par

He dealt and began explaining.\par

The game was simple enough to learn and difficult enough to distrust.
Each hand sought twenty-one without exceeding it.
Aces shifted between one and eleven, which Sa-Kailan found morally suspicious.
Equal results did not rescue the player; the Mountain won what would elsewhere have been called a tie.\par

“The Mountain wins ties?”\par

“Justice dislikes ambiguity.”\par

“Justice invented ambiguity.”\par

“Which is why they know how to charge for it.”\par

They played.
Sa-Kailan won early and was punished almost immediately.
Va-Raedin lasted longer, not because fortune loved him, but because fortune did not appear to know he was present.\par

Sa-Kailan ruined before half the shoe had gone.
Eventually Va-Raedin lost as well.\par

“The Mountain wins,” Sa-Kailan said.\par
“Does she?”\par
“She did.”\par
“This time.”\par
“And most times?”\par
“Yes.”\par
“Then why play?”\par
“That is the correct question asked too early.”\par

Ka-Syphiron squared the cards.\par

“A Veil of Justice, could double his token count during a shoe some of the time.
A Keeper, could play indefinitely, never ruin, and always accrue.”\par

“That is impossible.”\par

“Impossible saves lazy men the trouble of distinguishing what cannot happen from what cannot happen to them.”\par

“How?”\par

“Every hand has a correct action under condition.
The condition changes.
The action changes.
Perfect play delays ruin.
It does not abolish it.”\par

“Then how do they do it?”\par

Ka-Syphiron smiled.\par

“So how do they do it?” Sa-Kailan asked again.\par

The Keeper touched the discard pile.\par

“I will give you a clue.
The revealed cards are the past.”\par

"Extremely generous tonight, keeper." Va-Raedin said.

He touched the shoe.\par

“The remainder is the future.”\par

Sa-Kailan looked from one to the other.\par

“If the record of the past is kept perfectly, it alters the decision required for the future.
Not by prophecy.
Not by hope.
By condition.
What has already appeared changes what remains possible.
Justice knows this so deeply they have made morality from it.”\par

The cards sat between them like a small, silent tribunal.
Sa-Kailan almost understood.
Not enough.

No joke followed.\par

    % 24-Tomorrow


The evening changed.\par

No object moved.
No lamp dimmed.
Yet the laughter withdrew to the edges of the room, not banished, not denied, but completed.

“Tomorrow, the Mountain will speak what is already true.

“Yes.”\par

    % 25-Sa-Kailan leaves


Then Ka-Syphiron said, “All right.”\par

The words closed something.\par

“Sa-Kailan, I have a few things to tell Va-Raedin.”\par

There it was.
The door inside the door.\par

Sa-Kailan had been brought near.
Fed.
Praised.
Warned.
Taught beyond his level.
Allowed to laugh in the room where tomorrow had already begun.
But proximity was not possession.
Veil in fact was not yet Veil in office.
Some words still passed above him.\par

He rose.\par

For a moment he wanted to say something worthy of the evening.
Nothing came that would not diminish it.
So he bowed.
Lower than he had bowed on entering.\par

When he straightened, Va-Raedin was watching him.
The warmth was gone from his face, but not from the room.
It had simply taken the form Va-Raedin could bear.
Sa-Kailan turned toward the door.
He carried the prism in his hand and too many mysteries in the rest of him.\par

Faith as echo, prayer, and signal.
Balance as matter taught to obey conditions Light could only study once they existed.
Justice as future read by refusing to forget the past.
The Beast as the word men stopped joking about before the joke had finished forming.
And the memory of light, which even Ka-Syphiron had not pierced.
He opened the door.\par

The old gallery waited outside, dim and smoke-blackened.
Behind him, Ka-Syphiron and Va-Raedin did not speak until the door had nearly closed.
He heard one fragment.
Ka-Syphiron’s voice, lower than before.

“Here, for tomorrow and for ...”\par

Then the latch caught, and the rest belonged to those not yet dead and those not yet raised.
Sa-Kailan stood for a moment with his hand still near the closed door.
The corridor had not altered.
Everything in it had.\par

The public ceremony began before the Hall filled.\par

That was how it felt later, when he tried to remember sequence.
One moment he stood in the old gallery, carrying mysteries away.
The next, attendants of Light were dressing him in pale robes he had never worn before.
The folded white cloth was bound around his wrist, not like a wound, but like a visible condition.\par

He was led by Va-Raedin through corridors that had emptied ahead of them.
Shield had already closed side routes.
Balance had already marked warmth and lamp oil for the Hall.
Justice had already opened record.\par

The Mountain had begun to change around the fact.\par
