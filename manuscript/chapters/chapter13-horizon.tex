
\typesetchapter{Chapter 12}{Horizon}

\noindent Exception did not begin with movement.\par

It began with erasure.\par

Va-Kailan stood in the sealed route beneath the hearing chamber while Justice removed him from the ordinary sequence of the Mountain. He was not present to hear the public form of it, if public was the right word for a statement spoken before five witnesses in a room no family would enter. He heard only the lower version, given by the white-haired Justice witness while Shield prepared the transfer.\par

“Va-Kailan of Light ceases ordinary standing.”\par

The witness did not look at him when she read. That was mercy, or perhaps only discipline.\par

“Office consequence passes to Light under Keeper management. Household consequence passes to Faith under sealed wording. Material consequence passes to Balance. Movement consequence passes to Shield. Sequence consequence remains with Justice. The name is held from common use.”\par

Held.\par

Not killed. Not preserved. Held.\par

A name in the Mountain was not sound only. It was ration, bed, task, prayer line, training record, family place, work expectation, burial ledger. To hold a name was to lift it out of the channels through which a person remained among the living.\par

Va-Kailan looked at the dark cord Va-Sheva had tied below the covered white knot on his wrist. His Veil mark remained beneath Shield cloth. The Mountain did not remove what he was. It made what he was unusable.\par

“Will my mother be told I am dead?” he asked.\par

The witness turned one line on the tablet.\par

“Faith will give the household a form sufficient for survival.”\par

“That is not an answer.”\par

“It is the lawful answer.”\par

Va-Sheva stood three paces away, silent. No weapon showed on her. No explanation either.\par

“What form?” he asked.\par

The Justice witness looked at him then.\par

“One they can repeat without committing breach.”\par

He wished he had not asked.\par

A form they could repeat. Not truth. Not lie exactly. A shape designed for mourning without knowledge. His mother would receive words she could survive. His father would understand the absence of details better than most men and be ruined by that understanding. His household would not know whether to grieve death, disgrace, selection, or disappearance. Faith would stand with them. Faith would keep the wound from speaking too much.\par

That was mercy too, perhaps.\par

He was beginning to hate how often mercy arrived wearing the face of structure.\par

The transfer did not use public gates.\par

Shield took him through routes that had no childhood memory attached to them. Below the hearing chamber, below the old water lines, below even the places where workers joked that the Mountain had no further patience for stairs, the passages became narrower and less finished. Some were cut by tool. Some had been enlarged from natural seams. Some were so low that even Va-Sheva had to bend. The air moved strangely there: not Mountain air, warmed by bodies and lamps, but thin, dry streams that came from cracks the official maps of ordinary people did not require.\par

Balance had prepared a transfer pack.\par

It contained nothing sentimental. Dried fruit. Salted strips. Two water skins. A folded robe of rougher cloth. A mouth covering. Sand wraps. A small packet of bitter paste for the gums against thirst. A list of measures tied to the pack with green thread. Even exile had weight, water, salt, and inventory.\par

Va-Kailan noticed a second pack on one warden’s back.\par

“Is that mine too?”\par

Va-Sheva answered without turning.\par

“No. That is what Shield carries because Balance assumes you may become useless.”\par

He almost smiled.\par

Not because it was kind. Because it sounded like her, and there were not many things left that still did.\par

Justice stopped them at three points. Each time the witness marked his body, route, transfer condition, and silence. No one asked whether he wished to continue. Exception had already answered that question by removing its relevance.\par

At the last interior threshold, Faith waited.\par

Not Ka-Elun. Not Va-Maera. A single attendant of middle age with a covered face and a bowl of water in both hands. She did not know him, or had been chosen because she would not show whether she did. The bowl was shallow. The water inside did not tremble.\par

She spoke no scripture.\par

That omission hurt.\par

Instead she said, “What leaves under seal leaves under God.”\par

Va-Kailan lowered his head.\par

“What returns under seal returns only if God permits return.”\par

He looked up.\par

The attendant touched water to his forehead, then to the covered knot at his wrist, then to the dark cord Shield had tied beneath it.\par

“What is held from the people is not hidden from God.”\par

Her hands withdrew.\par

That was the whole blessing. Short, almost bare, shaped so that no forbidden fact entered holy language. Faith could mourn without knowing. Faith could bless without being told what it blessed.\par

The final Shield door opened outward.\par

Night waited.\par

The desert entered first as cold.\par

Then height.\par

Then smell: mineral, dry scrub, distant dust, something faintly animal beyond the prepared route. Va-Kailan stepped out beneath the Mountain’s shadow for the second time in his life and the first time without prayer access. The sky above him was full of stars. No vault. No ceiling. No cut stone receiving sound. Nothing to teach the eye where to stop.\par

He hated it less this time.\par

That frightened him.\par

The party moved before dawn. Va-Sheva led until the first ridge, then gave command to a Shield warden whose face had been wrapped so fully that only his eyes remained. Behind them came two more wardens and the Justice witness. No Faith. No Balance. Their parts had been completed at the threshold.\par

For a while the Mountain remained behind them, blacker than night. Then the path folded, and it vanished.\par

Va-Kailan stopped.\par

A warden touched his shoulder.\par

Not hard. Enough.\par

He turned back, but there was nothing to see. The Mountain had not moved. The world had simply arranged itself so that the only home he had ever known no longer existed from where he stood.\par

“Move,” Va-Sheva said.\par

He moved.\par

At first the journey was silence, cold, and pain in the feet. The sand wraps helped, but not enough. His body was trained for stone, stairs, chambers, controlled routes, measured heat. The desert had no interest in Mountain competence. It turned ankles, stole direction, widened distance, and made every ridge into a false promise.\par

When dawn came, Shield put them under stone.\par

Not a cave. A cut in the rock barely deep enough for bodies and packs. A cloth of desert colour was drawn across the outer edge and pinned with stones. From outside, they would become shadow. Inside, they became breath, heat, thirst, and waiting.\par

Va-Kailan was given two mouthfuls of water.\par

He wanted more and did not ask.\par

Va-Sheva sat near the opening, looking outward through a gap in the cloth. She had not removed her gloves. Dust had settled along the line of her cheek. Outside light found her face and made it older.\par

“Is this still the Mountain?” he asked.\par

“No.”\par

“Is it still Shield?”\par

“Yes.”\par

That distinction was almost the whole of his new life.\par

The day passed in fragments. Heat rose. The cloth breathed outward and inward with small changes in air. Once, a warden moved to adjust a stone and froze for three full breaths before finishing. Something had passed beyond the cut, unseen by Va-Kailan. Animal, bird, man, wind. Shield did not tell him. Shield did not narrate danger for the comfort of those protected from it.\par

Near evening, Va-Sheva gave him a strip of salted meat.\par

He took it.\par

“Did you know,” he asked, “before I showed you the scrap?”\par

She did not look at him.\par

“You are asking which part.”\par

“Yes.”\par

“I knew there were outer holdings.”\par

“Holdings.”\par

“Yes.”\par

“Archives?”\par

She said nothing.\par

“Contacts?”\par

Still nothing.\par

“Sarah Blackwood?”\par

Her face changed so little he almost missed it.\par

“You may not say that name again on this route.”\par

“But you know it.”\par

“I said you may not say it again.”\par

The old anger rose in him, but it found no easy shape. The trial had burned away the simplicity of accusation. She knew something. Not enough. Or enough but under seal. Perhaps knowledge in Shield came as passages did: not by map, but by station. One warden knew the outer marker. One knew the outcrop. One knew the route below it. One knew a name without a face. One knew a face without a name. One Veil might know there were holdings and still not have standing to name Horizon.\par

Let no one hand hold the whole.\par

Even guilt was divided.\par

“I thought knowing would make things clearer,” he said.\par

“That is because Light lies to its children differently from Shield.”\par

He looked at her.\par

“How does Shield lie?”\par

“We tell ours that if they train enough, they will see danger before it harms what they love.”\par

“And that is false?”\par

She turned from the cloth gap then.\par

“It is true often enough to destroy them.”\par

The answer silenced him.\par

At dusk they moved again.\par

The second night brought them farther from the Mountain than Va-Kailan knew how to measure. The stars wheeled overhead. Shield used them without naming them to him. The path avoided obvious valleys and easier ground, choosing instead hard shelves, dry cuts, stone ridges where footprints broke or vanished. Once they crossed a field of pale rock where every step seemed too loud. Once they lay flat while a distant engine passed beyond a ridge, a sound so alien that Va-Kailan first thought it some desert animal too large for scripture.\par

“What is it?” he whispered.\par

Va-Sheva’s hand closed once in warning.\par

He obeyed.\par

The engine faded.\par

Only later, when they moved again, she said, “Vehicle.”\par

The word belonged to no chamber he knew.\par

“Outsider?”\par

“Yes.”\par

“Did it see us?”\par

“No.”\par

He believed her because he had to. That dependency angered him more than ignorance.\par

Before the third dawn, the route ended at a dry well.\par

It looked abandoned. Stones fallen, lip cracked, rope long gone. A dead thorn tree leaned over it like a warning no one maintained. Shield circled the place before approaching. Va-Sheva inspected the inner wall with two fingers, found something, and pressed.\par

From below came a hollow answer.\par

Not a door opening. A message travelling through old depth.\par

They waited.\par

After a long while, a voice from inside the well spoke in a language Va-Kailan did not know.\par

One of the wardens answered in the same language.\par

The stone at the base shifted.\par

Horizon began as a hole beneath a dead well.\par

That disappointed him until he understood that disappointment was the child’s expectation of grandeur surviving where the man had no use for it.\par

The passage below the well was wider than Shield’s desert route, cooler, and prepared for repeated movement. Its walls were reinforced with outsider materials: metal ribs, smooth white panels, rubber-smelling seals, small glass bulbs that gave steady light without flame. Va-Kailan stopped before the first one despite himself.\par

“No lamp,” he said.\par

A man standing inside the passage laughed softly.\par

“Not in your sense.”\par

The accent was strange. The language was Mountain speech, but bent differently, as if each word had crossed water and returned changed.\par

The man was tall, broad-shouldered, and perhaps in his early forties. He wore desert-coloured clothes unlike Shield leather, fitted for movement but not ceremony. His hair was dark with grey at the sides. His face was lined by sun and discipline. A short blade sat at his belt beside stranger tools of metal and black material. He looked at the wardens, then at Va-Sheva, then at Va-Kailan.\par

“Jean-Charles,” Va-Sheva said.\par

The man inclined his head.\par

“Va-Sheva.”\par

There was history in the way he said her name.\par

Not romance. Not openly. Not yet. But recognition with more warmth than duty required. Va-Kailan saw it before he wished to see it, and because he saw it, something mean and young stirred in him even here, even now, beneath a dead well on the first route of exile.\par

He despised himself for it.\par

Jean-Charles looked at Va-Kailan and switched into more careful Mountain speech.\par

“So this is him.”\par

“This is the transfer,” Va-Sheva said.\par

“Of course.”\par

The correction was mild but received. He stepped aside.\par

“Welcome to the part of the world that pretends it is not also a cave.”\par

Va-Kailan did not answer.\par

Jean-Charles smiled as if silence amused rather than offended him.\par

“Good. Still alive inside the head, then.”\par

Va-Sheva said, “He is under Exception.”\par

“I know.”\par

“Do not test him for sport.”\par

“I never test for sport.”\par

The lie was so effortless that even the wardens seemed to ignore it by long practice.\par

They moved deeper.\par

At the first inner chamber, the Justice witness handed a sealed packet to a woman waiting beside a metal table. The woman had pale hair cut shorter than any Mountain woman’s, and eyes that assessed without asking permission. She wore grey, not the grey of Candidates but outsider cloth: jacket, trousers, boots, a narrow cord at the throat bearing no Kahir sign. Her face was neither young nor old in Mountain terms. She had the composure of someone who belonged to another hierarchy entirely.\par

“Elise,” Jean-Charles said.\par

The woman ignored him and took the packet.\par

Va-Sheva addressed her formally.\par

“Va-Elise.”\par

So the title remained here too, though its meaning had changed by crossing.\par

Va-Elise broke the outer seal under the witness’s eyes, read the first page, did not read the second, and signed a mark in ink on a smooth white sheet. Ink from a tube. Paper too perfect. The objects themselves were small shocks.\par

“Body received under Shield-Horizon transfer,” she said. “Speech restricted. Return unpresumed. Name held. Public standing ceased.”\par

The Justice witness answered, “Sequence delivered.”\par

Va-Elise looked at Va-Kailan for the first time.\par

“You understand your old name is not for ordinary use here.”\par

“No.”\par

“Good. Then listen. Va-Kailan belongs to the Mountain record. Kailan belongs to what you were before rank. Outside tongues will damage it immediately. For operational use, you will be Kylian. Within Horizon classification, because you are under instruction, not command, you are Sa-Kylian.”\par

The demotion entered more deeply than he expected.\par

“Candidate?”\par

“Provisional Candidate.”\par

“I was Veil of Light.”\par

Va-Elise’s expression did not soften.\par

“In the Mountain. Under a title now sealed from common use. Here you do not know how a door opens, how money works, how a public street is crossed, how a lie is printed, how a telephone betrays location, how a camera steals sight, how a church arranges memory, how a passport creates a person, or how an Outsider smiles while measuring whether you are useful. Candidate is generous.”\par

Jean-Charles murmured, “She likes to welcome people warmly.”\par

Va-Elise did not look at him.\par

“She should not be contradicted,” Va-Sheva said to Va-Kailan.\par

The sentence landed strangely because Va-Sheva was not defending Va-Elise. She was instructing survival.\par

Va-Kailan — no, Sa-Kylian, if the new grammar had already begun — looked at the table. There were objects arranged on it: folded clothes, dark shoes, a small book with his image not yet inside it, metal keys, a plastic card, a map printed in colours too precise to be a gravure. Beside them lay a pair of spectacles with clear lenses.\par

“I do not need those,” he said.\par

Va-Elise followed his gaze.\par

“They are not for correction. They are for concealment.”\par

“Of what?”\par

“Attention. People look differently at men with spectacles.”\par

That was absurd.\par

He looked at Jean-Charles. The man nodded gravely.\par

“Sadly true.”\par

The outer world was going to be worse than death in ways no scripture had prepared him for.\par

Va-Sheva removed the dark cord from his wrist. Not the covered white knot. The cord only. She gave it to Va-Elise, who placed it inside a small envelope and marked the time. The gesture made the transfer physical.\par

“Am I still under Shield?” Sa-Kylian asked.\par

Va-Elise answered before Va-Sheva could.\par

“You are under Horizon custody by Shield transfer and Justice sequence.”\par

“What is Horizon?”\par

“A line you cannot reach by walking toward it.”\par

Jean-Charles smiled.\par

Va-Elise continued, “That is the poetic answer. The practical answer is: the structure that keeps certain Mountain facts from dying inside the Mountain and certain outside facts from entering the Mountain uncontained.”\par

“Another Kahir?”\par

“No.”\par

Va-Sheva said, “Do not try to fit it into five hands.”\par

That frightened him more than the dead well.\par

The five hands had explained the world. Not wholly, not comfortably, but with a shape his mind could inhabit. If Horizon did not fit the five, then Horizon was either outside Covenant or built in a gap Covenant had prepared and hidden.\par

“Who commands it?”\par

Va-Elise closed the packet.\par

“For now, Ka-Sarah.”\par

Sarah.\par

The name returned before he could prevent it. Sarah Blackwood under desert light. The photograph. The record. The word Horizon sealed near her name.\par

He said nothing.\par

Va-Elise saw enough.\par

“You will meet her when you are clean, dressed, fed, and no longer looking as if every object in the room is a theological insult.”\par

“They are.”\par

Jean-Charles laughed.\par

Va-Elise’s mouth moved by a fraction.\par

“Good. He is not stupid.”\par

Va-Sheva said, “No.”\par

There was something in that no. Not pride. Not quite. Certainty. It reached him before he could defend against it.\par

They gave him water, food, and clothes.\par

The clothes were the first humiliation. Not because they were ugly, though they were. Trousers, shirt, jacket, underclothes shaped without modest ritual, socks, hard-soled shoes. They made him feel both exposed and disguised. Mountain robes arranged the body by office. These clothes arranged it by usefulness in a world of strangers.\par

Va-Sheva was not present when he changed. That was mercy.\par

When he returned, Jean-Charles looked him over and said, “Almost human.”\par

Sa-Kylian stared.\par

“In the outside sense,” Jean-Charles added.\par

“That does not improve it.”\par

“No. But you understood the insult. Progress.”\par

Va-Elise handed him the spectacles. He put them on. The clear lenses caught the white electric bulbs and placed small false lights at the edge of his sight.\par

“I hate them,” he said.\par

“They suit you,” Jean-Charles said.\par

Va-Sheva entered then, saw him, and stopped.\par

For the first time since the hearing, she nearly smiled.\par

Not enough for anyone else to accuse her of it. Enough for him to be wounded by the kindness.\par

“You look like an Outsider scholar,” she said.\par

“I have been called worse today.”\par

“Yes.”\par

The word changed the room again.\par

The transfer had reached its final personal moment and everyone there knew it. Va-Elise became suddenly occupied with the packet. Jean-Charles inspected a tool that required no inspection. The remaining warden looked at the wall.\par

Va-Sheva came closer.\par

“This is where Shield leaves you.”\par

“You leave me.”\par

“Yes.”\par

“Do you return to the Mountain?”\par

“Yes.”\par

“Will you tell them I reached Horizon?”\par

“No.”\par

“Will Ka-Raedin know?”\par

“Through sequence. Not through me.”\par

“And my family?”\par

She said nothing.\par

He looked away first. That was new. She had often looked away first when they were younger because she had somewhere to go and he had nowhere she permitted him to follow. This time he looked away because if he did not, he would ask for something neither of them could lawfully give.\par

“You did not choose death,” he said.\par

“No.”\par

“You chose this.”\par

“I chose the only containment that left you breathing.”\par

“That is not the same as choosing me.”\par

The sentence escaped before discipline could stop it.\par

Va-Sheva received it without visible movement. Jean-Charles became very still across the room. Va-Elise did not look up, which meant she was listening completely.\par

Va-Sheva answered quietly.\par

“No.”\par

It was cruel because it was exact.\par

Sa-Kylian nodded once.\par

“Then thank you for choosing breathing.”\par

Her face changed then. Pain, almost. Or anger. Or something too divided by office to survive in the open.\par

“You are alive,” she said.\par

“So I keep being told.”\par

“Believe it slowly.”\par

She stepped back before the moment became something Shield would have to contain.\par

Then she left.\par

No embrace. No private token. No promise. The door closed behind her, and with it the last person from the Mountain who had known him as Kailan before titles began cutting pieces from the name.\par

Sa-Kylian stood in outsider clothes beneath electric light and understood that survival had not brought him beyond punishment. It had brought him beyond the language in which punishment could be recognised.\par

They did not take him directly to Ka-Sarah.\par

First came instruction. Not in a chamber, but in a room filled with objects more dangerous for appearing harmless. A telephone. A camera. A printed newspaper. A map of the world, obscene in size. Coins and paper money. A passport with a blank place where his image would be placed. Va-Elise taught quickly and without pity. Jean-Charles interrupted only to translate the practical stupidity of things.\par

“A car is a small room that moves faster than a horse,” he said.\par

“I know what a vehicle is now.”\par

“No. You know the word. You do not yet know that roads are rivers made of decisions.”\par

Va-Elise said, “That one is actually useful. Remember it.”\par

They moved him again before dawn. This time not through desert, but by vehicle.\par

The first engine vibration made him grip the seat hard enough for Jean-Charles to notice.\par

“You are safer than you feel.”\par

“That is not a meaningful category.”\par

“Good answer.”\par

They crossed dark land, then roads, then places where outside lights multiplied without order. Towns. Buildings. Wires. Signs. Machines. People moving without visible permission. A world mad with doors and no wardens at them.\par

Sa-Kylian said little.\par

The journey after that became a sequence of enclosed transitions: vehicle, hidden room, aircraft, vehicle again, another room. The aircraft was the worst. To be above the earth, between Heaven and ground, held inside a metal body full of strangers who ate, slept, coughed, read, complained, and trusted height as if it were not blasphemy — that broke something ordinary in him. Jean-Charles sat beside him and said only, “Do not look down until I say.”\par

For once, Sa-Kylian obeyed without resentment.\par

They arrived in Switzerland in 1998.\par

He learned the date from a newspaper before he learned how cold the air would feel.\par

Switzerland was not the desert. That was the first betrayal of expectation. The outside was not one thing. It was mountains without secrecy, water without Covenant, roads cut openly through stone, houses exposed to sky as if sky were not a threat, churches with towers that pierced upward instead of hiding below. Snow remained on distant peaks though the lower ground had begun to soften. The air smelled of water, metal, pine, fuel, bread, and strangers.\par

They placed him in a house above a lake.\par

Not a prison.\par

That was its cruelty.\par

The windows opened. The doors had locks he could understand and others he could not. Food came in packages printed with languages. Water came by turning a metal handle. Light came by touching a switch. No one prayed when it appeared. No one measured the first cup. No one thanked the Mountain because there was no Mountain to thank.\par

For three days, Sa-Kylian barely slept.\par

On the fourth, Ka-Sarah received him.\par

She was older than the photograph and still recognisable enough that recognition became a form of vertigo. Sarah Blackwood had been young under desert light; Ka-Sarah was not. Her hair had gone mostly white, gathered at the back of her head. Her face carried age without surrendering command. She wore dark wool, not robes, and a small gold cross at her throat so discreet that it seemed more dangerous than display. Her eyes were blue-grey, cold, amused, and very tired.\par

The room where she waited had books on three walls, a map of North Africa behind glass, and a window overlooking the lake. No curtains were drawn. She sat with her back partly to the open view, as if no sane person would consider light from outside a tactical error.\par

Va-Elise stood beside the door. Jean-Charles near the window. No Va-Sheva.\par

Sa-Kylian did not bow. He did not know the correct movement.\par

Ka-Sarah observed him with open interest.\par

“It is not every decade that Horizon receives a Candidate,” Ka-Sarah said. “An ex-Veil, no less. How extravagant of Shield.”\par

The line was elegant enough to be insulting and amused enough to be worse.\par

“I was Veil of Light,” he said.\par

“Yes,” she said. “That is what ex means.”\par

He disliked her at once.\par

That seemed to please her.\par

“You are Sarah Blackwood.”\par

“I was Sarah Blackwood before most of the men who feared me learned how to spell it. Here, you may call me Ka-Sarah.”\par

“Keeper of Horizon?”\par

“Among other inconveniences.”\par

“Horizon is not a Kahir.”\par

“No. But Kahir grammar is hard to remove from children raised in caves.”\par

Va-Elise said nothing. Jean-Charles looked out the window with the expression of a man enjoying weather.\par

Ka-Sarah gestured to the chair before her.\par

“Sit. You are making exile look theatrical.”\par

He sat.\par

She studied him over folded hands.\par

“You have seen a room you were not meant to see, taken proof you were not meant to carry, survived a trial that had excellent reasons to kill you, crossed the desert, flown over Europe without screaming, and now you are in Switzerland wearing spectacles intended to make you look harmless. An eventful week.”\par

“Why am I here?”\par

“Because dead boys tell us less.”\par

“I am not a boy.”\par

“No. That appears to be part of the difficulty.”\par

She opened a file on the table. Paper, photographs, typed sheets, copied Mountain marks. His life had already become outside paperwork.\par

“You are under Exception. That means you are alive by procedure, not by freedom. Do not confuse the two. You will not leave this property without permission. You will not speak of the Mountain to anyone outside authorised Horizon structure. You will not attempt to contact your family, Ka-Raedin, Va-Sheva, or any Mountain office. You will not return toward the desert. You will not handle maps without supervision. You will not use telephones. You will not keep private writing materials until we decide whether your mind can hold paper responsibly.”\par

He almost laughed at that.\par

The sound did not come.\par

“And,” Ka-Sarah said, “you will not enter churches.”\par

That sentence landed differently.\par

“Why?”\par

Her eyes sharpened.\par

“Because you are too full of broken scripture, and churches are machines for arranging memory in public. You are not ready to stand inside one without either worshipping, condemning, or stealing from it. All three would be inconvenient.”\par

He thought of the tortured man on crossed wood. The father’s blade. The many-handed mother. The death-headed figure. The false water lord. He thought of Faith beneath stone and Ka-Elun’s voice reading Covenant. He thought of Va-Maera saying that God was not disproved by a valve.\par

“You wear a cross,” he said.\par

Ka-Sarah touched it lightly.\par

“Yes.”\par

“Are you one of them?”\par

“One of whom?”\par

“Outsiders.”\par

She smiled then.\par

“My dear boy, from where you sit, everyone is.”\par

He had no answer.\par

She closed the file.\par

“You think you have been taken beyond the Mountain. You have not. You have been moved to the Mountain’s horizon — the place where its shadow meets the world it refuses to name properly. You are not free. You are not dead. You are not restored. You are not yet useful. That is your present condition.”\par

“And if I refuse it?”\par

“Then Shield was right and Justice was merciful for too long.”\par

There was no cruelty in her voice. Only administrative fact.\par

Sa-Kylian looked past her, through the window, toward the lake. Water opened under sky without shame. Boats moved across it in full view. Houses stood along the shore, each with windows, lights, doors, names, people. No shadow of Mountain. No Oasis hidden from Heaven. No visible threshold between safety and the curse.\par

It should have looked like freedom.\par

It looked like exposure.\par

“What do you want from me?” he asked.\par

“For now? To survive the outside without turning every object into theology.”\par

“And after that?”\par

Ka-Sarah leaned back.\par

“After that, perhaps you will tell us what Light saw before Shield buried you.”\par

“You already know.”\par

“No,” she said. “We know what Shield could afford to transfer. We know what Justice could lawfully write. We know what Light is probably hiding from itself. We do not know what you saw.”\par

That sentence changed the room.\par

For the first time since the trial, his sight became something other than offence.\par

He distrusted the relief immediately.\par

Ka-Sarah saw that too.\par

“Good,” she said. “You are learning.”\par

He almost hated her for using the word in Ka-Raedin’s rhythm.\par

She stood, and the interview was over because she had decided it was.\par

“One more thing, Sa-Kylian.”\par

He looked at her.\par

“The Mountain will feel purer from here. Do not believe that. Distance flatters prisons and homelands equally.”\par

Then she left him with Va-Elise and Jean-Charles, in a room full of books he was not yet allowed to read, before a lake he was not yet allowed to approach alone, under a sky no one had measured for him.\par

That night, he lay in a bed too soft and listened to the house breathe.\par

No hidden pipes. No stone heartbeat. No distant gate chain. A heater clicked. A car passed below the road. Somewhere in the walls, an electrical system hummed with a steadiness he did not trust. The window showed lake darkness and scattered lights along the far shore. They trembled in the water like signals from a city that had no idea it was being watched.\par

He had been Kailan.\par

Then Sa-Kailan.\par

Then Va-Kailan.\par

Now Sa-Kylian.\par

A Candidate again, though not of Light. Candidate of Horizon, perhaps. Candidate of survival. Candidate of the outside. Candidate of not becoming proof that Shield should have chosen death.\par

He touched the place on his wrist where the white Veil knot had been covered. Horizon had not removed it. They had wrapped it beneath an ordinary sleeve. That was worse. A removed sign could be mourned. A hidden sign continued to accuse the skin.\par

He thought of his mother receiving a form she could survive. His father hearing what was not said. Ka-Raedin managing the office consequence. Va-Sheva returning through desert and stone to a Mountain that would continue without him. Ka-Sarah wearing a cross beneath books and maps. Jean-Charles smiling as if the world’s dangers had become personal acquaintances. Va-Elise turning exile into instructions.\par

Somewhere, perhaps not far in the strange geography of outside power, there would be a man named Paul Morgan.\par

Sa-Kylian did not know that yet.\par

He knew only that the Mountain had not ended at the Mountain.\par

Its shadow had crossed the desert, entered a dead well, passed into vehicles, aircraft, papers, houses, lakes, churches he was forbidden to enter, and names reshaped for foreign mouths.\par

The horizon was supposed to be where sight ended.\par

Here, it was where another custody began.\par
